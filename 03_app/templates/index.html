Visualizing Air Quality

An analysis of air quality trends in the U.S.

How has the COVID-19 pandemic affected air quality in the U.S.?

To find out, we gathered data from the EPA. We looked specifically at air quality index (AQI)



What is Air Quality Index?

AQI is EPAâ€™s index for reporting air quality. It is as a yardstick that runs from 0 to 500, with larger values representing higher levels of air pollution. AQI is determined by the follow air pollutants:

-Ground-level ozone

-Particle pollution (PM2.5 and PM10)

-Carbon monoxide

-Sulfur dioxide

-Nitrogen dioxide



Hypotheses

Null hypothesis

AQI has not improved in 2020, since the COVID-19 pandemic reached the U.S.

Alternative hypothesis

AQI has improved in 2020, since the COVID-19 pandemic reached the U.S.


Results

We found that average AQI for 2020 (January-May) was significantly lower compared to both 2019 (p-value=2.2824e-10 (station avg.) and p-value=1.0746e-08 (date avg.)).




The null hypothesis, is therefore rejected.

We could not determine, using only mobility data from Google and Apple, whether the lower AQI was due to decreased road traffic.

The visualizations below, however, show a clear dip in AQI beginnning in March 2020, which corresponds to a dramatic drop in mobility nationwide.

This data could be flawed. Updated and better data, and further research and testing is needed.
